Saturation Filter

The Saturation Filter began with me exploring around image processing. I was upset that a median filter is commonly used in the rejection of salt and pepper noise, and believed I can do better, with less damage to valid pixels (and thus loss/degradation of information).

 

The Saturation Filter runs a fast, crude spectral analysis to determine where in the spectrum a disturbance (mono-chromatic) is present. This is typically the case for high-radiation environments, which became the focus of this project.

 

 A 'selective median' filter is applied on pixels deemed to be 'damaged'. For high noise (n > 0.85) I found methods observing the histogram (for pixels creation rates) to be useful in predicting what the pre-corrupted image was, and reducing reconstruction error.

 

My filter did not create islands (as usually present on median reconstruction), at the cost of a longer compute time. I am still working on reducing compute time.

 

The example to the right uses a 900x900 grayscale bitmap with 90% of the pixels corrupted. Median filters would break down and not reject the noise, whereas my Saturation Filter recovers some fine-grain detail like the glint in Einstein's eye.

 

There are flaws, wisp structures begin to appear (as I sweep from left to right with my algorithm), and some islanding occurs at extreme noise conditions (n > 0.85).

 

For low noise, the image is damaged very little, and reconstruction is far superior to median filtration. This high-noise demonstration is just to show the algorithm is capable for high noise rejection.

 

The next stage for this project is to apply it to images from high-radiation environments, and even video, using priors to determine a good reconstruction.
